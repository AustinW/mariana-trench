H.M.S. Challenger (1874)

Expeditions have occurred since 1874, beginning with the H.M.S. Challenger. Led by Charles Thomson, the Challenger was originally a navy warship, however it had been outfitted with extra cabins, laboratories, and countless other scientific equipment. During the Challenger Expedition, almost 240 crew members participated in surveying the various depths of the Pacific Ocean. Armed with 181 miles of rope, they were able to painstakingly discover a 5 mile deep hole in the ocean floor. What they had discovered is known as Challenger Deep, the deepest point of the Mariana Trench. Of course, their scientific methods of surveying at the time provided incomplete results. Later, scientific advancements allowed for future expeditions to accurately survey the depth of Challenger Deep.

Trieste (1960)

In 1960, the Trieste, designed by Swiss scientist Auguste Piccard, permitted for a two manned expedition to the depths of Challenger Deep. Sonar (Sound Navigation And Ranging) allowed the team to accurately survey the depth and estimate the extreme pressure before designing the submarine, as the pressure was one of the primary concerns. On January 23, Jacques Piccard and Don Walsh became the first people to reach the full 35,814 ft depth. The descent lasted approximately four and a half hours at a rate of 3 ft/sec. Upon their journey, one of the windows cracked under pressure, rattling the submarine hull. Over-designed to withstand enormous pressure, the crack was unsettling. However, the pioneers agreed to carry on and experienced no further setbacks during their mission. At the ocean floor, the team immediately observed numerous small sole fish and flounder. This proved that vertebrate animals can survive under the outrageous pressure. Their find is important as the discovery could have application to humans and advance our knowledge of vertebrate structure. It could allow for advancements in science and medicine, potentially affording the ability to analyze the strength of backbones.

Canadian film director James Cameron navigated his way to the depths of the Mariana Trench in his custom designed Deepsea Challenger. While the Trieste blew away any doubt that life could exist at such depths, there was still much to learn about the organisms and microbes that lived there. James Cameron, with the help of his team of scientists, aimed to spend more time at the ocean floor to analyze as much as possible. Equipped with several HD cameras and sophisticated 3D imaging software, he did just that. In an interview with Cameron, he recounted:

We found bacterial mats in the Sirena Deep that appear to be getting their energy for life from a serpentinization reaction, which means they’re not dependent on food coming down from the surface from higher up in the water column. You’ve got an ocean environment that doesn’t need the sun, but life can be supported there. That could even be a model for how life emerged on this planet. At the Challenger Deep, not a lot of macrofauna down there. Flat bottom, very small stuff. But we did find this new sea cucumber, a lot of new microbes, and the science findings from even the tiniest bit of sediment that I was able to bring back has been very good.

It is clear from Cameron’s expedition that there were plenty of things he observed at Earth’s deepest location. He spent approximately three hours exploring the sea floor. However, there is more to be discovered and analyzed. The serpentinization reaction he referred to can be explained by Matthew O. Schrenk and William J. Brazelton:

The aqueous alteration of ultramafic rocks through serpentinization liberates mantle carbon and reducing power. Serpentinization occurs in numerous settings on present day Earth, including subduction zones, mid-ocean ridges, and ophiolites and has extended far into Earth’s history, potentially contributing to the origins and early evolution of life. Serpentinization can provide the energy and raw materials to support chemosynthetic microbial communities that may penetrate deep into Earth’s subsurface.

Biology

It is absolutely crucial that these organisms be able to survive without sunlight and sustain themselves at Earth’s deepest ocean floor. Information gleaned from Cameron’s findings can help gain an understanding of Earth’s early life and how it could evolve under the harshest of conditions. Data provided from these cells support theories claiming that life could have evolved from environments similar to that of the Mariana Trench. When plates slide past each other, methane and hydrogen are released (serpentinization), which could have provided an energy source for life to evolve.

In addition to microorganisms, other creatures were found at the depths of Challenger Deep during James Cameron’s three hour stay, as well as by scientists’ post analysis, including the aforementioned sea cucumber. The cucumber, similar to figure 3, was identified by Natalya Gallo after noticing a slight disturbance int eh sediment. When asked to describe it, she said, “It looks kind of like a fat worm with long feeding appendages.” In order to feed, they must situate themselves in such a way so that the current of the water delivers food particles directly to their feeding tentacles. Also, the Deepsea Challenger captured images of single-celled organisms called xenophyophores, as well as amphipods. Within the amphipods, scientists have discovered scyllo-inositol. The compound is useful because it is currently being studied for its potential in treating Alzheimer’s. Scientists have been analyzing the compound as a potential inhibitor for the progression of plaques on the brain. At the bottom of the ocean, it may be helping amphipods survive under extreme pressure.
